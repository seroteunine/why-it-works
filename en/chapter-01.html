Chapter 1

Voltage, Current, and Resistance

Saturday morning. A kettle lands on your table: dead, with no heat and no click. Before you open it, the question is simple: what should electricity be doing inside this kettle, and what would stop it? This chapter gives you the language for that question.

Learning goals
  • Understand the difference between voltage, current, and resistance in plain language.
  • Use Ohm's law to estimate what a normal appliance should be doing.
  • Recognise the difference between an open circuit, a short circuit, and a high-resistance fault.

The water analogy

Electricity is invisible, so it helps to borrow a picture from something visible: water flowing through pipes. The analogy is not perfect, but it is useful.

Voltage is the electrical equivalent of water pressure. It's the push — the force that drives current around the circuit. A higher voltage pushes harder. It's measured in volts (V), and the Dutch mains supply delivers 230V — enough pressure to push a lot of current through a lot of resistance. An AA battery, by contrast, delivers 1.5V. Same concept, much gentler push.

Current is the flow rate — how much charge is moving past a given point per second. Like flow rate in a pipe: litres per second for water, amperes (A) for electricity. A kettle heating element might carry 8 or 9 amperes during normal operation. A small LED might carry 20 milliamps (0.02A). Both are "current" — just very different amounts of it.

Resistance is the narrowness of the pipe — anything that restricts flow. A thin pipe offers more resistance than a wide one; a long pipe more than a short one. In electrical terms, resistance is a property of materials and geometry. It's measured in ohms (Ω). Copper wire has very low resistance — it gets out of the way. A heating element has high resistance — it deliberately restricts current, and converts the energy to heat in the process.

Ohm's law

These three quantities — voltage, current, and resistance — are related by one of the most useful equations in all of electronics:

V = I × R

Voltage equals current times resistance. Rearranged, it also tells you current from voltage and resistance, or resistance from voltage and current.

In practice: with a fixed supply such as 230V mains, the current depends on the resistance. Low resistance means more current. High resistance means less. An open circuit means no current at all.

In a kettle, the heating element is a coil of high-resistance wire. Its resistance is chosen so that at 230V it draws the right current and produces the right amount of heat. Measure the element's resistance, divide the supply voltage by that number, and you get the expected current. If that lines up with the rating — roughly 8 to 9 amps for a 2000W kettle — the element is probably healthy.

If the resistance reads OL (over limit) on a digital meter — meaning the resistance is too high to measure, effectively infinite — the element has failed open-circuit.

The underlying principle

Ohm's law works because many everyday materials behave predictably: double the voltage, and the current doubles too. That predictability is what makes fault-finding possible.

Voltage as a difference

Ohm's law treats voltage as a known number — 230V from the mains, say. But there's something important about what that number actually means. Voltage isn't a property of a single point. It's always a difference between two points. When we say "the mains is 230V," we mean there's a 230-volt difference between the live wire and neutral. When we measure voltage across a component, we're measuring the difference between one side and the other.

This matters when you measure. If you put one probe on live and one on neutral at the kettle element, you should read about 230V. If you read 0V, either the supply is missing or you're measuring between two points that are effectively the same.

From the workbench

When you're measuring voltage on a live circuit and you get an unexpected reading, the most common mistake is having one probe connected to the wrong reference point. Always ask yourself: what are my two measurement points, and what should the difference between them be? That reframing will save you a lot of head-scratching.

Current conservation: what goes in must come out

In a series circuit, the same current flows through every component in the loop. If a fuse, switch, and heating element are all in series, any current that reaches the element also passed through the fuse and switch first. That is why a break anywhere in the chain stops the whole appliance.

Resistance and where it comes from

Resistance depends on both material and shape. Copper wire has low resistance, so it gets out of the way. A heating element uses a material such as nichrome with much higher resistance, so electrical energy is turned into heat on purpose.

Geometry matters too. Longer wire means more resistance. Thinner wire means more resistance. That is why appliance cables are thick enough to stay cool, while the element is designed to get hot.

Temperature dependence

Resistance also changes with temperature. For most metals, hotter means higher resistance. So when you measure a cold element, you are looking for the right ballpark, not an exact number. Open circuit or near-zero resistance matters far more than small differences.

From the workbench

Kettle elements typically measure about 20 to 40 ohms cold. A quick check is 230² ÷ power rating. For a 2400W kettle, that gives about 22 ohms. OL suggests an open circuit; a very low reading suggests a short.

Open circuits, short circuits, and partial faults

Back to the kettle. Most electrical faults fall into one of three categories:

Open circuit: the current path is broken. Resistance is effectively infinite, current is zero, and the appliance does nothing.

Short circuit: two conductors that should be separate touch, creating a very low-resistance path. Current rises sharply, so fuses blow or breakers trip.

High-resistance fault: something still conducts, but worse than it should. The appliance may still run, but with reduced power or heat in the wrong place.

Safety

Probing an appliance while it's plugged in is only appropriate when you're specifically measuring voltage, you know what you're touching, and you're using a properly rated meter with insulated probes. Mains voltage across your body is 230V — enough to cause ventricular fibrillation. When in doubt, unplug first and use resistance measurements to narrow down the fault before going anywhere near a live circuit.

Putting it together

A working kettle is electrically simple: mains supply, series path, heating element, return. When it goes dead, something in that path has opened, shorted, or added too much resistance. The rest of the book gives you the tools to find where.

Back at the bench, the kettle is no longer just "dead." It is a circuit with a few likely failure points: the element, the thermal protection, the switch, or a connection between them.

Remember this
  • Voltage is the push, current is the flow, and resistance limits the flow.
  • At a fixed supply voltage, lower resistance means higher current.
  • OL on a resistance measurement usually means the current path is broken.
  • Most appliance faults can be described as open, shorted, or too resistive.

Check your understanding

Q1 You measure the resistance of a kettle's heating element. Your multimeter displays OL — meaning "over limit", the resistance is higher than the meter can measure. What does this tell you about the fault, and what would you recommend? Show answer

OL means there is no continuous path through the element: it is open circuit. The kettle will not heat, and the element usually needs replacing. Before concluding that, make sure you are really measuring the element itself and not an open thermal fuse or broken connection elsewhere in series.

Q2 A kettle element measures 28.75 ohms at room temperature. The mains supply is 230V. What current flows through the element when it's running? How much power does it convert to heat — that is, how many watts? Is this consistent with a kettle labelled "1800W"? Show answer

I = V / R, so 230 ÷ 28.75 = 8A. Power is 230 × 8 = 1840W, which is close enough to 1800W to be consistent with the label. Small differences are normal; a wildly different result would suggest a fault or a bad measurement.

Q3 A colleague says "I measured 230V at the plug, so the problem must be the element." What are they missing? Show answer

230V at the plug only proves the socket is live. The fault could still be in the cable, switch, thermal fuse, internal wiring, or the element itself. You have to trace the circuit, not jump to the last component.

Q4 In a simple series circuit (fuse → switch → heating element), is the current flowing through the fuse the same as or different from the current flowing through the element? Why? Show answer

The same. In a series circuit there is only one path, so the fuse and the element carry the same current. That is why the fuse can protect the whole appliance.

Q5 A kettle element measures 35 ohms on your meter at room temperature. The label on the kettle says 2000W at 230V. Should you condemn the element based on this reading? Show answer

No. The calculated hot resistance is about 26.5 ohms, but a cold element can read higher. A 35-ohm cold reading is still plausible for a kettle element. OL or a near-zero reading would be much more suspicious.
