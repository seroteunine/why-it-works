Before We Start

What's in here

Twelve chapters, roughly in the order they become useful to someone learning to repair.

Chapters 1–4 are the foundation: voltage, current and resistance, how circuits work, series and parallel connections, and power and heat. These chapters are about understanding — what electricity is, how it behaves, and why circuits work the way they do. If you're new to electronics, start here and work through them in order.

Chapters 5–12 go further: tools, components, and diagnosis. The multimeter, switches and fuses, thermal protection, motors, AC and DC, reading circuit diagrams, capacitors, and systematic fault diagnosis. These chapters are more applied — they build on the foundation and deal with the specific things you encounter at a repair cafe. Each one also stands on its own, so you can jump to whatever's relevant to what's in front of you today.

Each chapter opens with a moment from a repair cafe session: an appliance on the table, a problem to solve. Theory follows the practical situation, not the other way around.

How to use it

Read it front to back if you're new to the theory — the chapters build on each other. But each chapter also stands on its own, so you can jump to whatever's relevant to what's in front of you today.

Five kinds of callout appear throughout:

From the workbench

Practical observations — the kind of thing a more experienced volunteer might mention while standing next to you at the bench.

Safety

Read these carefully. Mains electricity can kill, and some appliances hold lethal charge even after unplugging. These boxes tell you when to stop and why.

The underlying principle

The physics behind what you've just read — not just what happens, but why. Skip them if you want; read them if you're curious.

Predict before measuring

A short prompt to form an expectation before you reach for the meter. If you know what a correct reading should look like, a wrong one becomes a diagnosis rather than a surprise.

Try it

Interactive elements you can click or manipulate to see how something behaves. No reading required — just try it and see what happens.

Each chapter ends with five questions — mostly applied, a few theory checks, and one that requires reasoning rather than recall. Click to reveal answers. Try to think first.

Safety and context

This book is written for the Dutch repair cafe context: 230V at 50Hz, Schuko plugs. Safety is covered throughout — not as box-ticking, but because understanding why something is dangerous is more useful than being told to be careful. The culture at a good repair cafe is disciplined rather than fearful: you check before you touch, you treat every appliance as live until verified otherwise, and when in doubt you ask. There is no prize for speed.

Units follow standard practice: ohms (Ω), amperes (A) or milliamps (mA), volts (V), watts (W). Formulas are explained in plain language before they appear in symbols. The appliances used as examples — kettles, toasters, vacuum cleaners, irons, fans — are the things that actually arrive at repair cafes. Chapter 1 starts with one of them.
