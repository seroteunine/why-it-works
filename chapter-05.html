Chapter 5

AC vs DC

The laptop charger goes on the table — a black plastic brick about the size of two decks of cards, with a cable from each end. "It stopped charging my laptop a few days ago," the owner says. She picks it up and squeezes it slightly. "It's warm. Is that normal?" You tell her it usually is, slightly. But the real question is whether it's charging. You plug it in with a meter in line. No output voltage at all. The cable from the brick to the laptop looks undamaged. The cable from the wall to the brick is fine. Something inside has failed. Before you open it — or decide not to — it helps to understand what's actually going on inside that little box.

Learning goals
  • Understand the difference between AC and DC in terms that help with repair.
  • Know why 230V mains has a peak of about 325V even though the meter reads 230V.
  • Recognise the main stages inside a simple power supply.

Direct current

Direct current, DC, is what batteries produce. The voltage is steady. Current flows in one direction, continuously. A AA cell is 1.5V DC. A USB power supply is 5V DC. The 12V in a car electrical system is DC from a battery (or from the alternator, but regulated to steady DC).

Most electronics — circuit boards, sensors, microcontrollers, LEDs, the logic inside your laptop — runs on DC. Specifically, low-voltage DC: 3.3V, 5V, 12V. The circuits are designed for stable, predictable voltages. Give them AC, or give them the wrong polarity, and things break.

Alternating current

Alternating current, AC, is what comes out of your wall socket. Instead of staying at a fixed voltage, AC swings back and forth: positive, through zero, negative, through zero, positive again. In the Netherlands (and across most of Europe) this happens as a sine wave repeating at 50 Hz — 50 complete cycles per second.

Peak voltage vs RMS voltage

Here's the part that surprises many people. When we say the Dutch mains is 230V, we don't mean the voltage is sitting at 230V — it's a sine wave that swings between about +325V and −325V. So why do we say 230V?

Because 230V is the RMS (root mean square) voltage — the effective value. RMS is defined specifically so that AC at X volts RMS delivers the same power to a resistive load as DC at X volts. A 230V RMS supply heats a resistive element (kettle, toaster, iron) identically to what a DC supply at 230V would.

Why does RMS get this special role instead of the peak value? Because heating depends on instantaneous power, and instantaneous power in a resistance depends on the square of the voltage at that moment. During each AC cycle the voltage is sometimes small, sometimes large, and sometimes zero. RMS is the mathematical way of averaging that changing voltage so that the average heating effect comes out right. It is the DC-equivalent heating value, not just an average of the waveform.

The relationship between peak voltage and RMS voltage for a sine wave is:

Vpeak = VRMS × √2     ≈     230 × 1.414 ≈ 325V

So the mains voltage is actually swinging between +325V and −325V. This matters for the design of capacitors and other components that must tolerate the peak voltage, not just the RMS. On mains circuits, voltage rating alone is not enough: components connected directly across line and neutral must also have the correct safety class for that job, such as an X-rated suppression capacitor.

From the workbench

When you set your multimeter to AC voltage and measure the mains, you'll read approximately 230V — that's the RMS value. If you were to connect an oscilloscope (or a peak-hold function) you'd see the full 325V peak. This distinction matters when you're testing circuit components. A capacitor marked only 250V is not suitable for direct use across the mains. For across-the-mains service you need the correct safety-rated part — typically an X2 capacitor of the specified capacitance and voltage class — not just "something with a big enough number on it."

Why mains is AC

The important practical reason is that AC is easy to transform from one voltage to another. That makes it suitable for a power system that must transmit energy efficiently at high voltage and then deliver it safely at household voltage. You do not need the whole history of the electricity grid at the repair bench; the useful takeaway is simply that the mains arrives as AC, while much of the equipment inside modern appliances needs DC.

For a resistive load, 230V RMS AC delivers the same heating power as 230V DC. The waveform differs; the effective power does not.

Turning AC into DC: rectification

Most electronic devices — phones, laptops, routers, many household appliances — need DC internally. The power supply's job is to convert mains AC to regulated DC. In broad terms, it does four things:

1. Transformation or isolation

The dangerous mains side and the low-voltage output side are separated and the voltage is brought to a useful range. In older supplies this is done directly with a mains-frequency transformer. In modern supplies the same job is often done in a different way, but the broad idea is the same: the appliance needs a safe, usable low-voltage supply, not raw mains.

2. Rectification

Convert AC to DC using diodes. A diode is a one-way valve for current — it conducts freely in one direction and blocks in the other. A bridge rectifier uses four diodes arranged so that both halves of the AC cycle push current through the load in the same direction. The output of a bridge rectifier is pulsating DC: it looks like a bumpy positive waveform, pulsing at 100 Hz (twice the mains frequency in a full-wave rectifier).

3. Filtering

Smooth out the pulsations using a large capacitor. The capacitor charges up on each pulse and partially discharges between pulses, filling in the dips. The result is a rippled DC voltage — close to steady, but with a small AC component superimposed. The bigger the capacitor, the smoother the output.

4. Regulation

Keep the output voltage constant despite varying load and varying mains input. This might be a linear regulator (simple, wastes energy as heat) or a switching regulator (complex, efficient).

That broad sequence — isolate or transform, rectify, filter, regulate — is the conceptual map to keep in your head. You do not need to know every internal design to understand what a charger or adapter is trying to achieve.

The underlying principle

Rectification depends on the one-way behaviour of diodes. A bridge rectifier uses four diodes so that both halves of the AC waveform end up pushing current through the load in the same direction. After that, filtering and regulation turn the bumpy output into something the electronics can actually use.

From the workbench

For this chapter, the important safety point is simple: the mains side of a power supply can contain dangerous voltage even after unplugging. The important diagnostic point is also simple: begin with the obvious. Damaged cables, cracked solder joints, failed output capacitors, and visibly burned parts are far more useful first observations than trying to understand every stage in detail.

Predict before measuring

If a bridge rectifier has failed open on one path, would you expect a smooth DC output, a lower and more rippled DC output, or zero volts? Try to predict the shape of the fault before you put the meter on the power supply.

The charger is opened. A visual inspection reveals a cracked solder joint at the input connector — probably from the fall. The joint is reflowed, the case is reassembled, and the output voltage checks correctly. The owner gets their charger back. You've now seen both sides of the AC/DC divide — the raw mains and the clean regulated output that most devices actually run on. Remember this
  • 230V mains is an RMS value; the sine wave peaks at about 325V.
  • Most power supplies follow the same broad path: isolate or transform, rectify, filter, regulate.
  • The mains side of a power supply can contain dangerous stored DC even after unplugging.
  • Across-the-mains capacitors must be replaced with the correct safety-rated type, not just a similar capacitance.

Check your understanding

Q1 A capacitor inside a mains power supply is marked "250V." The supply is 230V AC. Is this capacitor rated for the job? Show answer

No, not if it is connected directly across the mains. 230V AC is the RMS value; the peak is about 325V. A part marked only 250V would be overstressed. More importantly, mains-connected capacitors need the correct safety class as well as a suitable voltage rating. Across line and neutral that usually means an X-rated capacitor, such as X2, with the specified capacitance. If you find a 250V-only capacitor in that position, treat it as suspect until the circuit and part specification prove otherwise.

Q2 A charger is giving a low output voltage — say, 9V instead of the 19V rated. The mains input seems fine. Which broad part of the AC-to-DC process should you suspect first? Show answer

Start by suspecting the later stages: rectification, filtering, or regulation on the output side. Those are the stages that determine whether the final DC is clean and at the correct voltage. In practice, failed output capacitors or a fault in the regulation path are common explanations for a charger that still does something but no longer produces the correct output.

Q3 Why is the voltage difference usually more important than the 50 Hz versus 60 Hz difference for a simple resistive appliance? Show answer

Because the heat in a resistive load depends strongly on voltage. With a fixed resistance, power follows P = V²/R, so a large change in voltage gives a very large change in heating effect. The difference between 230V and 120V therefore changes appliance behaviour dramatically, while the difference between 50 Hz and 60 Hz is often secondary for simple heaters.

Q4 A bridge rectifier using four diodes is producing a pulsating DC output at 100 Hz from a 50 Hz input. Why 100 Hz rather than 50 Hz? Show answer

Because a full-wave bridge rectifier conducts on both halves of the AC cycle, not just one. In the positive half-cycle, two of the four diodes conduct current through the load in the positive direction. In the negative half-cycle, the other two diodes conduct — but they redirect the current so it still flows through the load in the positive direction. Every half-cycle (twice per full AC cycle) produces a pulse of current through the load. At 50 Hz, there are 100 half-cycles per second, so the output pulses 100 times per second. A half-wave rectifier (single diode) would only produce 50 pulses per second — which is harder to smooth and less efficient.
