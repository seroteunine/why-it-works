Chapter 5

AC vs DC

The laptop charger goes on the table — a black plastic brick about the size of two decks of cards, with a cable from each end. "It stopped charging my laptop a few days ago," the owner says. She picks it up and squeezes it slightly. "It's warm. Is that normal?" You tell her it usually is, slightly. But the real question is whether it's charging. You plug it in with a meter in line. No output voltage at all. The cable from the brick to the laptop looks undamaged. The cable from the wall to the brick is fine. Something inside has failed. Before you open it — or decide not to — it helps to understand what's actually going on inside that little box.

Learning goals
  • Understand the difference between AC and DC in terms that help with repair.
  • Know why 230V mains has a peak of about 325V even though the meter reads 230V.
  • Recognise the main stages inside a simple power supply.

Direct current

Direct current, DC, is what batteries produce. The voltage is steady. Current flows in one direction, continuously. A AA cell is 1.5V DC. A USB power supply is 5V DC. The 12V in a car electrical system is DC from a battery (or from the alternator, but regulated to steady DC).

Most electronics — circuit boards, sensors, microcontrollers, LEDs, the logic inside your laptop — runs on DC. Specifically, low-voltage DC: 3.3V, 5V, 12V. The circuits are designed for stable, predictable voltages. Give them AC, or give them the wrong polarity, and things break.

Alternating current

Alternating current, AC, is what comes out of your wall socket. Instead of staying at a fixed voltage, AC swings back and forth: positive, through zero, negative, through zero, positive again. This cycle repeats 50 times per second in the Netherlands (and across most of Europe). That's 50 Hz — 50 complete cycles per second.

The waveform follows a sine wave: a smooth mathematical curve that rises to a peak, falls back through zero, drops to a negative peak, and returns. Nothing jerky, no sharp edges. Just a continuous smooth oscillation.

Why 50 Hz? It's a historical engineering choice that stuck. The frequency needed to be high enough that incandescent lights didn't visibly flicker (below about 30 Hz they do), but low enough that generating and transforming it at scale was practical with early 20th-century technology. The USA settled on 60 Hz, the rest of the world mostly on 50 Hz. There's no deep physical reason — it's just standardisation.

Peak voltage vs RMS voltage

Here's the part that surprises many people. When we say the Dutch mains is 230V, we don't mean the voltage is sitting at 230V — it's a sine wave that swings between about +325V and −325V. So why do we say 230V?

Because 230V is the RMS (root mean square) voltage — the effective value. RMS is defined specifically so that AC at X volts RMS delivers the same power to a resistive load as DC at X volts. A 230V RMS supply heats a resistive element (kettle, toaster, iron) identically to what a DC supply at 230V would.

Why does RMS get this special role instead of the peak value? Because heating depends on instantaneous power, and instantaneous power in a resistance depends on the square of the voltage at that moment. During each AC cycle the voltage is sometimes small, sometimes large, and sometimes zero. RMS is the mathematical way of averaging that changing voltage so that the average heating effect comes out right. It is the DC-equivalent heating value, not just an average of the waveform.

The relationship between peak voltage and RMS voltage for a sine wave is:

Vpeak = VRMS × √2     ≈     230 × 1.414 ≈ 325V

So the mains voltage is actually swinging between +325V and −325V. This matters for the design of capacitors and other components that must tolerate the peak voltage, not just the RMS. On mains circuits, voltage rating alone is not enough: components connected directly across line and neutral must also have the correct safety class for that job, such as an X-rated suppression capacitor.

From the workbench

When you set your multimeter to AC voltage and measure the mains, you'll read approximately 230V — that's the RMS value. If you were to connect an oscilloscope (or a peak-hold function) you'd see the full 325V peak. This distinction matters when you're testing circuit components. A capacitor marked only 250V is not suitable for direct use across the mains. For across-the-mains service you need the correct safety-rated part — typically an X2 capacitor of the specified capacitance and voltage class — not just "something with a big enough number on it."

Why we use AC for mains power

The honest answer is: transformers. A transformer converts AC voltage from one level to another with almost no energy loss. Step up the voltage for long-distance transmission and you need far less current for the same power — which means far less I²R loss in the cables. Step it back down at the destination for safe household use. There's no efficient equivalent of a transformer for DC, which is why AC won and the grid is still built around it.

And why does stepping up the voltage help so much? Because for a given power, higher voltage means lower current. Since cable heating rises with the square of the current, halving the current cuts transmission losses to a quarter. That is the whole economic logic of the AC grid: move energy around the country at high voltage and modest current, then transform it down only where people actually use it.

Tempting but wrong

"230V AC must be gentler than 230V DC because the voltage keeps passing through zero." For a resistive load, 230V RMS AC delivers the same heating power as 230V DC. The waveform differs; the effective power does not.

Turning AC into DC: rectification

Most electronic devices — phones, laptops, routers, many household appliances — need DC internally. The power supply's job is to convert mains AC to regulated DC. This conversion has several stages:

1. Transformation

Step the mains voltage down (or up) using a transformer. In older, heavier power supplies, a traditional iron-core transformer does this at 50 Hz. In modern switched-mode power supplies, it's more complex (see below).

2. Rectification

Convert AC to DC using diodes. A diode is a one-way valve for current — it conducts freely in one direction and blocks in the other. A bridge rectifier uses four diodes arranged so that both halves of the AC cycle push current through the load in the same direction. The output of a bridge rectifier is pulsating DC: it looks like a bumpy positive waveform, pulsing at 100 Hz (twice the mains frequency in a full-wave rectifier).

3. Filtering

Smooth out the pulsations using a large capacitor. The capacitor charges up on each pulse and partially discharges between pulses, filling in the dips. The result is a rippled DC voltage — close to steady, but with a small AC component superimposed. The bigger the capacitor, the smoother the output.

4. Regulation

Keep the output voltage constant despite varying load and varying mains input. This might be a linear regulator (simple, wastes energy as heat) or a switching regulator (complex, efficient).

What you've just described — transformation, rectification, filtering, regulation — is inside every charger, power adapter, and wall-wart you'll encounter. The process is always the same four stages. Once you know them, a dead power supply stops being a mystery and becomes a checklist.

The underlying principle

Rectification exploits the asymmetric behaviour of semiconductor diodes. A silicon diode conducts in the forward direction (positive side higher than negative) with a forward voltage drop of about 0.6V, and blocks reverse current almost completely. A bridge rectifier (four diodes) ensures that regardless of which half-cycle the AC is in, current is always directed through the load in the same direction. This is the standard approach for all mains power supplies. The "1N4007" marked on the diodes you'll see on many circuit boards is a common 1A rectifier diode rated to 1000V — suitable for rectifying mains voltage safely.

Switched-mode power supplies

The charger brick for your laptop doesn't contain a large, heavy iron transformer. It contains a switched-mode power supply (SMPS), and understanding the basic idea explains why these chargers are small, light, and efficient — and also why they fail in specific ways.

In an SMPS, the mains AC is first rectified to high-voltage DC (about 325V peak). This DC is then very rapidly switched on and off through a small transformer — at frequencies tens of thousands of times higher than the mains. At high frequency, transformers can be tiny: the size of a transformer scales with how slowly the voltage alternates, so a transformer running at a high switching frequency can use a ferrite core the size of a coin instead of the heavy iron core you'd need at 50 Hz.

The high-frequency AC from the switching stage goes through the small transformer, is rectified again on the secondary side, filtered, and regulated. This is why the charger on your table is light, compact, and warm rather than hot — the efficiency is far higher than older transformer-based designs.

From the workbench

The most common SMPS failure modes at the repair table are: a blown primary-side fuse (often indicating a failed switching transistor or short on the primary side), a failed startup resistor, swollen or failed electrolytic capacitors on the output side (causing high ripple and low output voltage), and physical damage to the cable. Testing an SMPS requires caution — the primary side contains voltages up to 400V DC, not mains AC, and large capacitors that hold charge after unplugging. This particular charger went on a drop test: the most likely culprit is a broken solder joint on the input connector or a cracked component. Visual inspection first, always.

What the buzz in a transformer tells you

Transformers sometimes hum or buzz audibly. The hum frequency is twice the mains frequency — 100 Hz for a 50 Hz supply — because the magnetic core expands and contracts twice per cycle (once per half-cycle, regardless of polarity). A small amount of hum in a large transformer is normal.

A transformer that suddenly starts humming much louder than before, or buzzing with a harsh sound, is usually telling you one of three things: the core laminations have loosened (mechanical), the transformer is being overloaded (magnetic saturation), or there's a partially shorted winding. A harsh buzz that gets hotter is usually a winding fault. A hum that's proportional to load is usually saturation or loose core. Neither is something to ignore.

Predict before measuring

If a bridge rectifier has failed open on one path, would you expect a smooth DC output, a lower and more rippled DC output, or zero volts? Try to predict the shape of the fault before you put the meter on the power supply.

The charger is opened. A visual inspection reveals a cracked solder joint at the input connector — probably from the fall. The joint is reflowed, the case is reassembled, and the output voltage checks correctly. The owner gets their charger back. You've now seen both sides of the AC/DC divide — the raw mains and the clean regulated output that most devices actually run on. Remember this
  • 230V mains is an RMS value; the sine wave peaks at about 325V.
  • Most power supplies follow the same broad path: rectify, filter, regulate.
  • Primary-side power-supply work can involve dangerous stored DC even after unplugging.
  • Across-the-mains capacitors must be replaced with the correct safety-rated type, not just a similar capacitance.

Check your understanding

Q1 A capacitor inside a mains power supply is marked "250V." The supply is 230V AC. Is this capacitor rated for the job? Show answer

No, not if it is connected directly across the mains. 230V AC is the RMS value; the peak is about 325V. A part marked only 250V would be overstressed. More importantly, mains-connected capacitors need the correct safety class as well as a suitable voltage rating. Across line and neutral that usually means an X-rated capacitor, such as X2, with the specified capacitance. If you find a 250V-only capacitor in that position, treat it as suspect until the circuit and part specification prove otherwise.

Q2 A charger is giving a low output voltage — say, 9V instead of the 19V rated. The mains input seems fine. What stage of the SMPS is most likely at fault? Show answer

Low output voltage with the mains input fine most commonly indicates a failure in the output stage: failed or high-ESR electrolytic capacitors (causing high ripple and poor regulation), a failed output rectifier diode (losing voltage in the rectification), or a fault in the feedback regulation circuit (the circuit that monitors output voltage and adjusts the duty cycle). Swollen or leaking electrolytic capacitors on the secondary side are a very common cause. If the output voltage is stable but low, the regulation circuit is running but can't achieve the correct voltage — possibly because the feedback reference or error amplifier has drifted. If the output voltage is erratic or very ripply, suspect the output capacitors.

Q3 Why do appliances designed for 230V/50Hz often work on 60Hz (North American) systems, but not at 120V? Show answer

Power consumption — and the heat produced in resistive elements — depends on the square of the voltage (P = V²/R). An appliance designed for 230V run at 120V would receive only (120/230)² ≈ 27% of its rated power. A kettle would take much longer to boil. A motor designed for 230V run at 120V would draw less current than intended — it might not have enough torque to start. Frequency (50 vs 60 Hz) affects things like motor speed (which is tied to supply frequency) and transformer core sizing, but most modern devices handle the 50/60Hz difference. Voltage is what really matters for power delivery. That's why "universal" SMPS chargers specify a voltage range (e.g. 100–240V) explicitly.

Q4 A bridge rectifier using four diodes is producing a pulsating DC output at 100 Hz from a 50 Hz input. Why 100 Hz rather than 50 Hz? Show answer

Because a full-wave bridge rectifier conducts on both halves of the AC cycle, not just one. In the positive half-cycle, two of the four diodes conduct current through the load in the positive direction. In the negative half-cycle, the other two diodes conduct — but they redirect the current so it still flows through the load in the positive direction. Every half-cycle (twice per full AC cycle) produces a pulse of current through the load. At 50 Hz, there are 100 half-cycles per second, so the output pulses 100 times per second. A half-wave rectifier (single diode) would only produce 50 pulses per second — which is harder to smooth and less efficient.

Q5 A customer brings a US appliance rated 120V/60Hz containing only a simple resistive heating element (no motor, no electronics). They've been using it in the Netherlands with only a plug adapter — no voltage converter. What has most likely happened to the element, and why? Show answer

Power in a resistive element is P = V²/R. The element's resistance is fixed, so power scales with voltage squared. At 230V instead of 120V, the power ratio is (230/120)² ≈ 3.67. The element was designed for, say, 500W; at 230V it's attempting to dissipate nearly 1840W — almost four times its rated power. The element would glow far hotter than designed, almost certainly burning out within seconds or minutes. If the appliance had a thermal fuse (many US appliances do), it may have also tripped or blown. The 50/60Hz frequency difference doesn't matter for a purely resistive element, but the voltage difference is catastrophic. A plug adapter changes the connector shape; it does nothing to convert the voltage. This is a surprisingly common cause of instant appliance death.
