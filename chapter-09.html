Chapter 9

AC vs DC

A table fan arrives with a simple complaint: "It hums a bit, but it doesn't really start." Next to it is a battery-powered radio that has simply gone dead. These two jobs are not the same kind of electrical problem. One lives in the world of mains AC, motor windings, and capacitors. The other lives in the world of low-voltage DC. You do not need a whole course in power electronics to handle that distinction, but you do need to know which world you are in before you start measuring.

Learning goals
  • Understand the practical difference between AC and DC.
  • Know what the multimeter is really showing when it reads 230V AC.
  • Recognise when AC/DC knowledge matters for a repair cafe job, and when it mostly does not.

Direct current

Direct current, DC, is what batteries provide. The voltage stays at one polarity and current flows in one direction. Small battery appliances, simple electronics, and the low-voltage side of adapters all live in this world.

At a repair cafe, DC usually matters when you're dealing with battery-powered devices, adapters, control boards, LEDs, or any circuit that has already converted the mains to a lower voltage. The practical point is not to become an electronics specialist; it is to recognise that these parts of an appliance want steady low-voltage DC, not raw mains AC.

Alternating current

Alternating current, AC, is what comes out of your wall socket. Instead of staying at a fixed voltage, AC swings back and forth: positive, through zero, negative, through zero, positive again. In the Netherlands (and across most of Europe) this happens as a sine wave repeating at 50 Hz — 50 complete cycles per second.

The important picture is that AC is not electricity turning on and off. It is voltage reversing direction over and over again. The push first drives charge one way, then the other. That is why AC can power heaters directly, but motors and electronics sometimes need extra components to make use of it properly.

What matters about mains AC

Here's the part that matters at the bench. When we say the Dutch mains is 230V, we do not mean the voltage sits at a flat 230V. It swings above and below zero, reaching about +325V and −325V on each cycle.

The 230V your meter shows is the RMS value — the effective value. For a resistive load such as a kettle, toaster, or iron, 230V RMS AC produces the same heating effect as 230V DC would.

That is why the mains number on the label remains useful even though the waveform is moving all the time. For practical repair work, RMS is the number that tells you what the supply can actually do in terms of heating and power. The peaks matter for insulation and component ratings; the RMS value matters for ordinary circuit behaviour.

The relationship for a sine wave is:

Vpeak = VRMS × √2     ≈     230 × 1.414 ≈ 325V

You do not need this because most repair-cafe jobs involve waveform calculations. You need it for two simpler reasons: to understand what your AC voltage reading really means, and to remember that components on the mains side must survive the peak voltage, not just the number printed on the socket.

From the workbench

When you set your multimeter to AC voltage and measure the mains, you'll read approximately 230V — that's the RMS value. If you were to connect an oscilloscope (or a peak-hold function) you'd see the full 325V peak. This distinction matters when you're testing circuit components. A capacitor marked only 250V is not suitable for direct use across the mains. For across-the-mains service you need the correct safety-rated part — typically an X2 capacitor of the specified capacitance and voltage class — not just "something with a big enough number on it."

When this chapter matters

For many repairs in this book, AC/DC is not the main event. A dead kettle, faulty lamp, burned switch, failed fuse, or open heating element can often be understood perfectly well with the ideas from the previous chapters: voltage, current, resistance, circuits, and power.

This chapter matters when one of three things is true: you must choose the correct AC or DC setting on the multimeter, you are working near the mains side of an appliance and need to think clearly about voltage and safety, or the appliance contains a motor, capacitor, adapter, or control board where AC is being used differently from simple resistive heating.

Where AC becomes DC inside an appliance

Some appliances are almost entirely mains AC machines: kettles, toasters, irons, simple fans. Others contain a small section that converts the mains to lower-voltage DC for controls, timers, displays, or electronic switching. You do not need to know the full internal design in order to repair most of them. What matters is recognising that an appliance may have both worlds inside it: mains AC in one part, low-voltage DC in another.

The broad conversion path is simple enough to remember: AC comes in, rectification turns it into one-direction current, capacitors smooth it, and the circuit uses that DC where needed. That is enough background for this book. If you later open an appliance and find a control board, display module, or adapter section, you will at least know why AC has disappeared and DC has appeared.

The underlying principle

Rectification depends on the one-way behaviour of diodes. They allow current through in one direction and block it in the other. Arranged properly, they turn an alternating waveform into a one-direction flow that capacitors can smooth into usable DC.

From the workbench

Do not let the presence of a small circuit board convince you that every repair has become an electronics job. Start by asking what the appliance fundamentally is. If it is mainly a heater, motor, switch, fuse, or cable problem, stay with that. Use the AC/DC distinction mainly to choose your meter setting and to avoid getting lost when a low-voltage section appears.

Predict before measuring

You measure a battery pack on AC mode and get nonsense. You measure a live mains socket on DC mode and get nonsense. What is the first explanation in both cases? Before blaming the appliance, check whether you are asking the meter the right question.

The fan on your table turns out to have a failed run capacitor, while the radio simply needed its supply traced on the DC side. The useful lesson is not that every repair depends on AC/DC theory. It is that when AC/DC does matter, it usually matters in a small number of practical ways: what your meter should be set to, what part of the appliance you are really looking at, and what voltage may still be present even after unplugging. The next step is to turn that understanding back into circuit structure on paper.

Remember this
  • Mains appliances usually start in the world of AC; batteries and many control circuits live in the world of DC.
  • 230V mains is an RMS value; the sine wave peaks at about 325V.
  • For many repair-cafe jobs, AC/DC is a supporting idea rather than the main diagnostic tool.
  • The main practical uses of this chapter are meter selection, safety awareness, and recognising when an appliance contains both AC and DC sections.

Check your understanding

Q1 Why might a multimeter show a strange or useless reading even when the appliance is fine? Show answer

Because the meter may be on the wrong kind of measurement. Measuring AC with the DC setting, or DC with the AC setting, often gives a misleading number or no sensible number at all. Before drawing conclusions about the appliance, first check that the meter is set to the kind of electricity actually present at that point in the circuit.

Q2 Why do we say the mains is 230V when the waveform actually reaches about 325V peak? Show answer

Because 230V is the RMS value, which is the effective heating value of the AC supply. For a resistive load, 230V RMS AC produces the same heating effect as 230V DC would. The waveform itself swings higher and lower than that, but the meter reading is reporting the effective value rather than the peak.

Q3 A kettle is a simple resistive appliance. Why is AC/DC knowledge usually not the main thing you need to diagnose it? Show answer

Because most kettle faults can already be understood with the earlier chapters: is the circuit complete, what is the element resistance, is the switch intact, is the thermal protection open, is there voltage at the right place? The AC/DC distinction is background knowledge here, not the main diagnostic path.

Q4 When is AC/DC knowledge especially worth keeping in mind at a repair cafe table? Show answer

When you must choose the meter setting correctly, when you are thinking about mains-side safety, or when an appliance contains both a mains section and a low-voltage control section. Those are the moments where knowing whether you are in AC or DC territory stops confusion and prevents unsafe assumptions.

Q5 An appliance has an internal power supply that converts 230V AC to 12V DC for a control board. The appliance is completely dead. You measure 0V DC at the 12V output rail. You then set the meter to AC and measure the input side of the transformer: also 0V. The wall socket is confirmed live. Where does this pattern point, and what would you check next? Show answer

The pattern shows that 230V AC is present at the socket but not reaching the transformer inside the appliance. The fault is in the AC section, before the conversion point — not in the DC section or the control board. Something is breaking the mains supply path between the plug and the transformer primary. The most likely candidates are: a blown appliance fuse (always in series in the AC section), the main switch, or a broken wire or connector in the AC wiring before the transformer. Check the fuse first with continuity — it's the quickest test and the most common cause. If the fuse is intact, trace voltage forward from the plug with the appliance connected: 230V AC before the fuse, nothing after it means the fuse is the fault even if it looked intact visually. This case illustrates why the AC/DC distinction matters for diagnosis: a dead control board is often caused by a failure in the mains section upstream of it, not a failure of the board itself.
