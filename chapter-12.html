Chapter 12

Systematic Fault Diagnosis

It's mid-afternoon. The repair cafe is winding down. Three appliances remain on the table — two repaired and waiting to be claimed, one still open: a coffee maker with a circuit board visible and several components marked with your little sticky-dot system. You've been at this table since nine this morning. Over the course of the day, you've fixed what you could, referred what you couldn't, and learned something from each. Now, in the last hour of the session, it's worth stepping back and thinking about how you approached all of it — because the method matters as much as the knowledge.

Learning goals
  • Approach faults systematically instead of by guesswork.
  • Build a safe first-pass workflow for dead, partial, and intermittent faults.
  • Know when to stop, explain honestly, and refer the repair.

The problem with random part replacement

There's a temptation in repair work — especially when you've seen many faults — to recognise a symptom and jump to the most likely culprit. Sometimes this is right. But it's a pattern that leads to wasted parts, wasted time, and occasional made-worse appliances when the jumped-to part wasn't the problem.

Systematic fault diagnosis is the discipline of not guessing. It's the practice of working through a logical sequence of tests that eliminates possibilities one by one, until you've identified the fault with the minimum number of measurements. It's slower at first. With practice, it becomes faster than guessing — because you stop replacing parts that turn out to be fine.

Before anything else: is this safe to work on?

Every appliance that arrives at your table needs a quick safety assessment before any diagnosis. This takes thirty seconds and should become habit:

  • Is the cable or plug physically damaged? Damaged cables should be replaced before the appliance is returned to service, regardless of whether they're causing the fault.
  • Is there evidence of water or liquid ingress? Wet internals may have compromised insulation in ways that aren't immediately visible.
  • Is the earth connection intact (for Class I appliances with metal cases)?
  • Is there evidence of fire damage, excessive burning, or explosive failure of a component? If so, the cause needs to be understood before anything is powered up.
  • Does this appliance fall into a category requiring special care — microwave, large CRT TV, power tool with damaged safety guard?

If the answer to any of these is "yes" or "unsure," the safety question must be resolved first. Nothing is fixed until it's safe.

Safety

When in doubt about whether an appliance is safe to power up for functional testing, do your electrical tests first with the appliance unplugged. Continuity and resistance measurements can tell you a great deal without ever connecting to the mains. If you do need to test with power applied, have a proper test environment: know where the supply is and how to cut it instantly, keep one hand away from the circuit (so you can't accidentally provide a path across your chest), use insulated probes, and have someone else present if possible. Never work alone on live mains circuits with no one aware of what you're doing.

The diagnostic sequence

Step 1: Understand the symptom precisely

Ask the owner: what does it do? What does it not do? Did it fail suddenly or gradually? Was there a sound or smell? Has anything changed recently — a drop, a liquid spill, a new location? What was it doing when it failed?

The owner's account is evidence. A sudden death with a bang and a smell is different from a gradual weakening over months. "It was fine yesterday" usually means a component failure. "It's been getting worse for a while" means wear or degradation. "It stopped when I moved house" might mean a voltage issue (though unlikely in the Netherlands where voltage is standardised), or physical damage in the move.

Step 2: Visual inspection (unplugged)

Open the appliance and look before you touch anything. You're looking for:

  • Obviously failed components: blown fuses, burst capacitors (bulging/vented tops), burned resistors, scorch marks
  • Physical damage: cracked PCBs, broken solder joints (look for joints that are cracked or have a "volcano" shape — dry solder joints), disconnected wires
  • Contamination: liquid residue, corrosion on contacts, carbon deposits from arcing
  • Mechanical wear: worn brushes, damaged gears, broken springs

A thorough visual inspection often finds the fault without any electrical testing. Don't rush it. Look at every component and every connection. Use a torch if the area is dark. About 30% of the faults you'll encounter can be identified visually.

Step 3: Identify the circuit structure

Before you measure anything, understand (or sketch) what you're looking at. What are the series elements (in the path of all current)? What are the parallel branches (each serving a specific function)? If you have a schematic, use it. If not, trace the main current path from plug to load.

Step 4: Divide and conquer

The most efficient diagnostic strategy is binary division: test the midpoint of the circuit first. If the midpoint is fine, the fault is in the second half. If it's not, the fault is in the first half. Then test the midpoint of the faulty half. In a few steps, you've narrowed a 20-component circuit down to one suspect.

In practice, the "divide" is often logical rather than geometric: test the mains supply chain (fuse → switch → thermal protection) first, since these affect everything. Then test each parallel branch individually. Within a branch, test from the supply end toward the load end.

The important mental shift is this: every measurement is a test of competing explanations. Before you measure, name the fault stories that could explain the symptom. Then choose the measurement that will eliminate the most stories at once. A reading is useful not because it gives you a number, but because it makes some explanations impossible and others more likely.

What you've just described — narrowing the fault location by testing at logical midpoints — is the same method experienced repairers use on every job. Not because they memorise a checklist, but because they've internalised the question: where in this circuit would a fault explain this symptom? When that question becomes automatic, the sequence follows naturally.

Tempting but wrong

"More measurements automatically mean better diagnosis." Not if the measurements are random. Ten unplanned readings can tell you less than two well-chosen ones. The goal is to collapse uncertainty, not to collect numbers.

From the workbench

For a completely dead appliance (no lights, no sound, no movement), the diagnostic path is almost always: fuse → main switch → thermal protection device → power supply (if applicable) → load. These four stops cover the vast majority of "dead" faults. For an appliance with partial failure (one function dead, others working), start at the branch point — what's common to the working functions? Work backwards from there. For intermittent faults (works sometimes, not others), the path is harder: flex and wiggle cables while testing continuity, look for dry solder joints, test for thermally-dependent faults by letting the appliance warm up and then cool down while monitoring the symptom.

Using your measurements strategically

Voltage testing (live circuit)

In a live circuit, trace voltage from the supply toward the load. Voltage present up to a component, absent after it: the component is open. Full voltage where there should only be a fraction: something upstream is open, and all the voltage is dropping across the open gap instead of being shared.

One powerful technique: the voltage-drop test. In a correctly working series circuit, resistance is low and voltage drop is low. A poor connection, corroded contact, or failing component will drop more voltage than it should. Measure across each series element in turn. The one with a measurable voltage drop when there should be none is the problem.

Resistance testing (unplugged)

Test components individually, in isolation if possible (disconnect one end to remove parallel paths that give false readings). Know what you expect before you measure: calculate the expected resistance from the appliance's power rating, look up the specification if you have a service manual, or compare to a working example of the same appliance.

Continuity testing (unplugged)

For cables, switches, fuses, and connections, continuity is the fastest check. Beeps mean conducting, OL means open. Remember the caveat: continuity passes at low current, but a high-resistance partial break may still have problems under full load.

The underlying principle

Systematic diagnosis is essentially the application of circuit theory to an unknown fault. Kirchhoff's voltage law tells you what voltage you should measure at every node in a working circuit. Kirchhoff's current law tells you what current should flow through every branch. Ohm's law gives you the resistance implied by any voltage/current pair. When your measurements deviate from these predictions, the deviation tells you what has changed — and changed components are faults. The physics doesn't change; only the component has. The discipline is in making enough measurements to triangulate the location of the deviation.

Common fault patterns by symptom

Completely dead — no response to power

Work through: wall socket supply (is the socket live, and has the automaat or aardlekschakelaar in the groepenkast tripped?), appliance fuse if present, main switch, thermal cutoff, main power supply. 80% of "completely dead" faults are in this chain.

Works but overheats

The thermostat or thermal protection isn't tripping as it should (thermostat stuck closed, wrong calibration), or heat is being generated where it shouldn't be (bad bearing, high-resistance connection, blocked ventilation). Check the thermal protection cycle and the appliance's ventilation before looking at the electronics.

Intermittent — works sometimes, not others

Usually mechanical: a loose connection, a dry solder joint that makes and breaks with vibration or temperature, a switch with worn contacts. The diagnosis is: reproduce the fault condition (flex cables, tap the body, wait for it to warm up) while monitoring with a continuity measurement or voltage measurement. The moment the fault occurs, you'll see it in the measurement.

Trips the breaker or blows the fuse

There's a short circuit or serious overcurrent somewhere. Test with the appliance unplugged: check resistance from live to neutral — should be the load resistance (maybe 20–200 ohms for typical appliances). Near zero or zero ohms means a short circuit. Disconnect components one by one until the short disappears — the last disconnected component was the culprit.

Noisy but functional

Usually mechanical: worn brushes (sparking noise), bad bearing (grinding), loose mechanical parts (rattling), or electromagnetic noise from a failing component (buzzing, clicking). Physically locate the noise source while the appliance runs — at a safe distance and with appropriate caution about moving parts.

Reduced performance

Either the load isn't receiving full power (high-resistance path in the supply chain, failing switch contacts, corroded connections) or the load itself has degraded (partially failed heating element with some sections open, motor with worn brushes or shorted windings). Test the current draw if possible: compare to rated specification. Higher than rated suggests a fault in the load (motor winding issue). Lower than rated suggests a high-resistance supply chain reducing available power.

Predict before measuring

If an appliance is completely dead but one indicator lamp still works, what fault stories are immediately less likely? Ask that before the next measurement. The best prediction is often about which explanations have already been eliminated.

When to stop

Not every fault is repairable at a repair cafe. Knowing when to stop is as important as knowing how to diagnose.

Stop and refer when:

  • The fault is in a high-voltage circuit (microwave, CRT TV, large capacitor banks) and you're not confident in the safety procedures
  • The fault requires specialist parts that aren't available and the repair cost approaches or exceeds replacement cost
  • The appliance has a safety-critical function (gas control valve, carbon monoxide detector) and you're not certain of the repair
  • PCB-level faults require component-level diagnosis and soldering skills beyond what's appropriate to attempt
  • The appliance is inherently end-of-life (certain motor types with non-replaceable bearings, fused heating elements with no part availability)

An honest "this isn't repairable here" is a good outcome. The owner knows what's wrong, understands why repair isn't the answer, and makes an informed decision about replacement. That's a service too.

From the workbench

Make a brief note on the repair ticket for every job, even the ones that fail: what you found, what you tested, what your conclusion was. This helps the next person who might see the same appliance, or the same fault pattern in a different brand. It also helps you remember what you've learned. A pattern you recognise on the third occurrence is a skill. A pattern you only see once and never record is a missed opportunity. The shared knowledge in a repair cafe is one of its most valuable assets — maintain it.

The repair cafe philosophy

By this point in the session, you've diagnosed more than a dozen faults. Some were simple and quick. Some required thought. One or two are going away unresolved. What you've been doing all day is applying the same set of underlying principles — Ohm's law, series and parallel circuits, thermal physics, component characteristics — to different contexts, different appliances, different failure modes.

The appliances matter less than the mental model. Once you can think in terms of loops and series chains and voltage drops and current paths, an unfamiliar appliance is just an unexplored map of the same territory. You won't always know the answer immediately. But you'll know how to find it.

The repair cafe's value isn't just in the appliances that get fixed. It's in what happens to the people in the room while it's happening — the conversation, the transfer of knowledge, the moment when someone who brought in a toaster they thought was beyond saving goes home with it repaired and a slightly different relationship with the things they own. The understanding that things can be understood. That "why it works" has an answer.

The session ends. The coffee maker with the open circuit board gets labelled "to be continued" and left with the coordinator for next time. The tables are wiped down. The multimeters go back in their cases. You pick up your bag and look at the repair log for the day: ten appliances, seven repaired, two referred, one recycled. Not a bad Saturday. See you next week.

Remember this
  • Start with safety, symptom, and visual inspection before deeper testing.
  • Work from the supply path toward the load, dividing the problem as you go.
  • Measurements are only useful when you know what result you expect.
  • Stopping and referring is part of competent repair, not a failure.

Check your understanding

Q1 An electric kettle is completely dead. You've confirmed the wall socket is live. Describe your diagnostic sequence, in order, without touching a single component. Show answer

First: visual inspection unplugged — look at the cable for damage, the plug for signs of burning, and inside the kettle body for obvious failures (burned parts, disconnected wires, obvious thermal damage). Then, working from the supply toward the load: (1) Confirm the wall socket supply separately. (2) Check cable continuity — both conductors from plug to the kettle's internal connections should be intact. (3) Check the main switch — continuity with switch in the on position. (4) Check the thermal fuse / thermal cutoff — continuity; if open, the kettle has overheated and needs investigation before replacement. (5) Check the heating element — resistance, typically around 20–40 ohms depending on wattage. OL = open element, primary fault. Each step either passes (move to the next component) or fails (that component is the fault).

Q2 A toaster trips the automaat in the groepenkast the moment it's plugged in — before the lever is even pressed. What does this symptom tell you, and how do you proceed? Show answer

Tripping the breaker before the switch is pressed means a short circuit exists in the wiring that's always connected when plugged in — before the switch has engaged the heating elements. The line and neutral conductors are effectively shorted together somewhere accessible from the mains entry, without going through the switch. Candidates: the cable itself (insulation damaged, conductors touching), the plug terminations, or the filter components at the inlet if the toaster has an EMI filter (for example a failed X-capacitor across the mains). Proceed unplugged: check resistance from line to neutral at the plug or mains entry — it should be OL or very high with the switch open. Near zero means a short in the always-energised part of the circuit. Check cable, plug, inlet wiring, and inlet components.

Q3 An appliance works normally when first switched on, but after 10–15 minutes fails completely. After it cools for 30 minutes, it works again. What type of fault does this pattern suggest, and what would you look for? Show answer

This is a classic thermal-intermittent fault. Something in the circuit fails when it reaches operating temperature and recovers when cooled. Possibilities: (1) A dry solder joint that makes good contact cold but loses contact when the PCB or component expands with heat — look for cracked solder joints, especially on large components or connectors that see mechanical stress. (2) A thermal cutout with a trip temperature that's too low for this application, or that has drifted — it trips after sustained operation and resets after cooling. If it's an NTC thermistor used as an overtemperature sensor, it may be changing value in a way that triggers protective shutdown. (3) A failing component whose electrical characteristics degrade unacceptably with temperature. The diagnostic approach: reproduce the failure by letting it run, then immediately test continuity through the protection chain and check voltages at key points before it recovers. The measurement that deviates while the fault is present is the lead.

Q4 You've repaired a toaster by replacing a blown fuse. It works in testing. Should you return it to the owner? What, if anything, should you say? Show answer

It depends on whether you've understood why the fuse blew. A fuse doesn't blow from old age — it blows because it saw excessive current. If you replaced the fuse and it stayed intact during testing (a few minutes of normal operation), you haven't proven the fault is resolved — you've only confirmed the fault isn't occurring right now. Before returning it, you should have: checked the element and thermal protection for any abnormality that could have caused the fuse to blow; confirmed the fuse was the correct rating (not an over-rated replacement someone fitted previously); and confirmed the appliance runs normally through a full cycle. If all checks are clear and the fault doesn't recur in testing, return it with an honest explanation: "We replaced the fuse and everything tested normal, but if the fuse blows again, bring it back — it might mean there's another fault we haven't found yet." This is honest, helpful, and sets appropriate expectations.

Q5 You've replaced a broken heating element in an electric blanket (Class I, 3-core cable). It heats correctly in testing. What specific checks must you complete before returning it, and why does this appliance warrant extra caution beyond a standard kettle repair? Show answer

Specific checks: (1) Earth continuity — measure from the earth pin on the plug to the appliance chassis/element outer sheath. A Class I appliance with a broken earth must not be returned, and for a blanket, the user may be lying on it. (2) Inspect the full cable length for pinching, kinking, or any damage — blanket cables flex constantly in use and are a common failure point beyond the element itself. (3) Confirm the thermostat cycles correctly at the right temperatures — a blanket thermostat that runs high causes burns, not just spoiled fabric. (4) Confirm the TCO (if present) is intact and correctly rated. The extra caution is warranted because an electric blanket operates in direct contact with the body for hours while the user is asleep and unable to respond to overheating. A missed fault that would be merely inconvenient in a kettle — a slightly high temperature, a degraded connection — can cause burns or worse in a blanket. The stakes of incomplete checking are higher than in most appliances on the repair cafe table.
