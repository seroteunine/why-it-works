Before We Start

Who this book is for

This book is for repair cafe volunteers and hobby fixers who already have some hands-on experience — people who've sat down at a repair table, unscrewed the bottom of a vacuum cleaner, replaced a fuse and handed something back that worked again. Not beginners who've never touched a screwdriver, and not electronics engineers who already know Kirchhoff's laws. The practical middle ground: real hands-on experience, but the theory hasn't quite clicked into place yet.

The goal isn't to turn anyone into an electronics engineer. It's to build a working mental model — one that leads to better guesses at the workbench, better questions, and a real understanding of why the things that work actually work. That understanding makes repair work faster, safer, and more confident.

What's in here

Twelve chapters, roughly in the order they become useful to someone learning to repair. We start with the fundamentals — voltage, current, resistance — and build outward from there into circuits, power, AC and DC, the multimeter, protection devices, motors, and finally a systematic approach to fault diagnosis.

Each chapter begins with a moment from a repair cafe session — a Saturday morning, an appliance on the table, a problem to solve. The theory follows from the practical situation, not the other way around. By the end of chapter 12, the session has run its course.

There's also a reference appendix at the back: a multimeter quick-reference, common circuit symbols, and a fault-finding checklist you can print and keep at your station.

How to use it

Read it chapter by chapter if you're new to the theory — the chapters build on each other, and the story thread runs from start to finish. But the book is also designed to be dipped into. Each chapter stands on its own reasonably well, and the navigation lets you jump to whatever's relevant to what's in front of you today.

The callout boxes are intentional interruptions. There are three kinds:

From the workbench

Green boxes ground the theory in practical observation — the kind of thing a more experienced volunteer might mention while standing next to you at the bench.

Safety

Red boxes are the ones to read carefully. Mains electricity is genuinely dangerous, and some of the appliances you'll encounter — microwave ovens especially — can hold lethal charge even after they're unplugged. These boxes tell you when to stop, what to watch for, and why certain procedures exist.

The underlying principle

Grey boxes zoom out. They explain the physics or engineering principle behind what you've just read — not just what happens, but why. You don't need these to understand the practical content, but they'll deepen your understanding if you want it.

At the end of each chapter, there are four questions. Most are applied — the kind that start with "you open a toaster and see..." — with a few theory checks mixed in. Click to reveal the answers. Try to think before you click.

A note on safety culture

Repair cafes work with mains-powered appliances. 230 volts at 50 Hz — the standard Dutch grid supply — can kill. That's not a disclaimer; it's a practical fact that should shape how you work.

The safety culture at a good repair cafe is neither fearful nor casual. It's disciplined. You check before you touch. You don't work on live circuits unless you're specifically measuring voltage and you know what you're doing. You discharge capacitors before probing. You treat every appliance as potentially live until you've verified otherwise.

This book covers safety where it's relevant — not as a box-ticking exercise but because understanding why something is dangerous is more useful than just being told to be careful. When you understand what 230V across your heart does, you don't need a warning label to make you respectful of it.

If you're ever unsure whether something is safe to open and test: ask a more experienced volunteer. That's what the repair cafe model is for. There is no prize for speed, and no shame in stopping.

Context and conventions

This book is written for the Dutch repair cafe context. Mains voltage is 230V at 50Hz. Plugs are Schuko (Type F) — the round two-pin plug used across much of continental Europe. Where specific voltage or frequency matters, those numbers are the reference point.

Resistances are in ohms (Ω), currents in amperes (A) or milliamps (mA), voltages in volts (V), power in watts (W). When we write formulas, we'll explain them in plain language first.

The appliances used as examples throughout — kettles, toasters, vacuum cleaners, coffee machines, irons, fans, grinders, milk frothers, headphones — are the things that actually arrive at repair cafes. They're familiar. That's the point.
