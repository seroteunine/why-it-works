Chapter 6

Switches and Fuses

The vacuum cleaner bag is so full it's turned to a solid brick. "It just stopped working," the owner said when it arrived — which is what everyone says. You empty the bag, clear the hose and filter, and reassemble. You plug it in — and nothing. No hum, no attempt to start, nothing. The motor isn't even trying. You press the power switch a few times. Still nothing. With an overfull bag, the machine may have been running hot for a while — some vacuums have thermal protection that trips when they overheat. But on this model, there's also a small glass fuse visible inside the machine. You reach for the multimeter.

Learning goals
  • Understand what switches and fuses actually do in a mains appliance.
  • Recognise why fuse type matters just as much as fuse current rating.
  • Use a blown fuse as a clue without mistaking it for the root cause.

What switches actually do

A switch is mechanically simple but electrically precise: it creates or destroys a conducting path. In the on position, the switch's contacts touch — low resistance, current flows. In the off position, the contacts separate — infinite resistance, current cannot flow.

What happens in that instant of opening is easy to miss. The contacts do not go from perfect touch to infinite separation in one clean step. For a brief moment they are very close together while current is still trying to flow, and the electric field across the tiny air gap can become strong enough to ionise the air. A miniature arc jumps the gap. That arc is why switch contacts slowly burn, pit, and wear out.

The key rule from Chapter 2 bears repeating: switches should interrupt the intended live conductor, or both conductors in a double-pole design. That reduces the chance of leaving the load sitting at mains potential when "off." In Schuko systems, because plug orientation is reversible, good appliance safety should not depend on plug orientation alone.

Types of switches in appliances

SPST (Single Pole Single Throw): the simplest type. One input, one output. On or off. The rocker switch on a kettle, the on/off on a fan — these are SPST.

SPDT (Single Pole Double Throw): one input, switches between two outputs. Used for heat settings — "low" connects the motor to a different circuit element than "high." The three-speed switch on a hair dryer is typically two SPDTs.

DPST/DPDT: double-pole types interrupt both live and neutral simultaneously. Used on some appliances where full isolation of the load is required — some old kettle switches, some workshop equipment. Both poles must make and break together.

Microswitches: precision mechanical switches with a small lever or plunger. They provide a very reliable snap action — the contacts make and break quickly, which reduces arcing. Common in safety interlocks (lid switches on washing machines, door switches on microwave ovens), door-close detectors, and limit switches in mechanisms.

Push-to-make / push-to-break: momentary switches — contacts are only in one state while you're pressing them. A doorbell button is push-to-make (circuit closes while pressed). Some espresso machine switches use push-to-break for safety cutoffs.

From the workbench

The most common switch failure in appliances is worn or pitted contacts. The arc that occurs every time contacts open under load (especially at mains voltage) gradually erodes the contact surface. Eventually, the contact resistance rises, the switch heats at the contact point, and either starts exhibiting intermittent failures or arcs so badly it welds closed. A switch that feels "gritty" or takes two or three presses to operate, or an appliance that's intermittently dead, is often a contact problem. Check resistance across the closed switch: should be near zero. A few ohms indicates worn contacts.

Fuses: how they work and why they exist

A fuse is a sacrificial component: a small element of wire or strip designed to melt and open the circuit when the current through it exceeds its rated value for long enough. Once it blows, it's destroyed — it must be replaced.

The fuse element is calibrated so that:

  • Normal operating current flows through it indefinitely without melting
  • A moderate overcurrent (say, 150% of rated) melts the element after some time
  • A large overcurrent (fault current, short circuit) melts it in milliseconds

The fuse doesn't know what's causing the overcurrent — motor stall, short circuit, wiring fault, component failure. It simply responds to the current. Its job is to protect the wiring and reduce fire risk before damage spreads.

A blown fuse is usually the messenger rather than the cause. Something else demanded too much current, and the fuse responded exactly as designed.

You've just understood why a protection device has to carry the current it's protecting against — and therefore why it must always be in series. Once that logic is clear, the placement of every fuse, thermal cutoff, and circuit breaker you'll encounter makes immediate sense.

The underlying principle

The fuse element melts when Joule heating (I²R) raises its temperature to its melting point. Because the element has high resistance relative to the wiring around it (it's thin), it heats faster than anything else in the circuit under overcurrent. The material is chosen for a specific melting point (often a tin-lead or silver alloy) so that the fusing current and time characteristics are predictable. This time-current characteristic is the key design parameter: a fast fuse blows quickly at even a small overcurrent; a slow (time-lag) fuse tolerates short-duration overcurrents (like motor startup spikes) but still blows on sustained faults.

Fuse types

Fast-blow (F) vs time-lag (T)

Fast-blow fuses (marked F) operate quickly at rated current. They're used in circuits that must be protected immediately — sensitive electronics, circuits with no startup current transients.

Time-lag fuses (marked T, also called anti-surge or slow-blow) tolerate a brief overcurrent before blowing. They're used in circuits with inductive loads — motors, transformers — that draw several times their running current when they start. A motor that draws 3A running might draw 15A for half a second on startup. An F-rated fuse would blow every time it started. A T-rated fuse tolerates the startup transient and only blows if the overcurrent is sustained.

This distinction matters at the repair table. If an appliance has a T-rated fuse and someone replaces it with an F-rated fuse of the same current value, the appliance will blow the fuse every time it starts. The solution is the correct fuse type, not a higher current rating.

Physical types

Glass cartridge fuses: the 5×20mm (or 6.3×32mm) glass tube fuses common in European appliances and equipment. You can often see visually whether the element is intact by holding the fuse to the light. If the glass is blackened, it blew under a heavy fault. If the glass is clear but the element is visibly open, it blew more gently.

Ceramic cartridge fuses: same form factor as glass, but with a ceramic body. Used where higher breaking capacity is required — the ceramic body can contain the arc of a high-fault-current blowing. Required for higher-voltage or higher-power circuits.

PCB fuses: small glass or ceramic fuses soldered onto circuit boards, typically in SMD or through-hole packages. Often the first thing to check on a dead PCB.

Fuse ratings

A fuse has three critical ratings:

  • Current (A): the continuous current it can carry without blowing. A 1A fuse must carry 1A indefinitely; it blows at sustained overcurrent above that value.
  • Voltage (V): the maximum circuit voltage the fuse can safely interrupt. Exceeding this can mean the arc doesn't extinguish when the element melts — the fuse "fails conducting" rather than clearing the fault.
  • Breaking capacity (A): the maximum fault current the fuse can safely clear. A low-breaking-capacity fuse may fail violently if asked to clear a large short-circuit current.

For household appliance repair, glass cartridge fuses rated at 250V with breaking capacities of 35–200A are normal. Always replace with the same type and rating, not just the same physical size.

Safety

A blown fuse is evidence of a fault, not the fault itself. The fuse has done its job — it has detected overcurrent and broken the circuit. Replacing the fuse without investigating why it blew is not a repair: it's an experiment to see whether the fault recurs. Sometimes the answer is useful (the fuse blows again immediately, telling you there's a short circuit) but often it just delays diagnosis and risks damage. Before replacing a blown fuse, check for visible damage, short circuits, and failed components. Only replace the fuse once you have a theory for why it blew and have addressed the underlying cause.

Circuit breakers

Circuit breakers serve the same protective function as fuses but are resettable. The domestic MCB (Miniature Circuit Breaker) in the groepenkast uses two mechanisms:

Thermal trip: a bimetal strip carries the load current. When current is sustained above the rated value, the strip heats and bends, eventually tripping the breaker. This takes seconds to minutes depending on how much above rating the current is. It mimics the behaviour of a time-lag fuse.

Magnetic trip: a solenoid carries the current. A fault current (short circuit) produces a strong magnetic field that instantly trips the breaker. This happens in milliseconds, before significant damage can occur.

MCBs are rated by type as well as current: Type B trips magnetically at 3–5 times rated current (for lighting and general socket circuits), Type C at 5–10 times (for motor circuits and small commercial loads), Type D at 10–20 times (for large motors and very inductive loads).

Predict before measuring

If a fuse is intact, what should your meter read across it with the appliance unplugged: near zero ohms or OL? And if it is blown, which reading should appear instead? Make that call before you test.

From the workbench

Back to the vacuum cleaner. The fuse is a 3.15A T-rated glass cartridge. Hold it up to the light: the element is visibly open, and there's slight blackening of the glass — suggesting it blew under a reasonable fault current rather than just old age. With an overfull bag, the motor was under heavy load for a sustained period. It may have drawn enough sustained overcurrent to blow the thermal fuse; the glass fuse may have blown secondarily, or it may have blown because of a motor winding partially shorted from overheating. Check the motor resistance before fitting a new fuse and switching on. If the motor winding reads much lower resistance than expected, there may be a partial short — fitting a new fuse and powering up will just blow it again.

The vacuum's motor checks out within normal range. A new fuse fitted, and the machine runs. The owner is reminded to empty the bag before it reaches capacity. You make a note on the ticket: thermal stress suspected — check motor carefully at next service. The steam iron that's been on the side table is next. Someone said the temperature control seems off — it's scorching cotton at a linen setting. That's a thermostat problem, and thermostats are the next thing to understand.

Remember this
  • A switch opens or closes a current path; a fuse sacrifices itself to stop overcurrent.
  • Same amperage is not enough: fast and time-lag fuses are not interchangeable.
  • A blown fuse is evidence that something abnormal happened.
  • Replacing a fuse without understanding why it blew is diagnosis, not repair.

Check your understanding

Q1 An appliance specifies a T2A fuse (time-lag, 2A). Your stock only has F2A fuses (fast-blow, same 2A rating). The appliance contains a motor. Does the type matter, and what would happen if you fitted the F2A? Show answer

Yes, the type matters significantly. Motor circuits draw several times their running current for a fraction of a second on startup. A 2A motor might draw 8–10A briefly before the rotor gets up to speed. A T2A (time-lag) fuse is designed to absorb this short energy pulse without blowing, then protect against sustained overcurrent. An F2A (fast-blow) fuse at the same current rating will blow on the startup transient every time the appliance is switched on — even though nothing is wrong. The result: an appliance that seems unrepairable because it keeps blowing fuses, when the real problem is just the wrong fuse type. The current rating (2A) and the fuse type (T vs F) are separate specifications and both must match the original.

Q2 A hair dryer has a three-speed switch. On "low," it barely blows. On "medium," it blows weakly. On "high," it works normally. What does this pattern suggest? Show answer

On the low and medium settings, the circuit introduces a series resistance or switches the motor to a reduced-voltage configuration. If both lower settings give weak output, the common element is likely a series resistor that's supposed to reduce voltage for low and medium speeds. A resistor that's increased in resistance (carbon composition resistors can do this as they age) would drop more voltage than intended, leaving less for the motor. Alternatively, the switch contacts for those positions may be worn, adding contact resistance. Check the resistance of the speed-control components on those settings and compare to the specified values. The "high" setting usually bypasses the resistors entirely, which is why it works normally.

Q3 A glass cartridge fuse blows again immediately after replacement. What does this tell you, and what should you do next? Show answer

Immediate re-blowing means there's a sustained short circuit or severe overcurrent fault present in the circuit — the fuse is clearing a real fault, not a transient. The fault is still there. Do not fit a third fuse. Disconnect the load from the circuit and check component by component: look for short circuits (zero or near-zero resistance between live and neutral), failed components that draw abnormal current (shorted motor windings, failed capacitors, shorted semiconductors), and visible damage (burn marks, melted insulation). Use resistance measurement across each component (with circuit disconnected) to identify what's drawing excessive current. Only when the fault is identified and corrected should a replacement fuse be fitted.

Q4 Why do motor circuits often use time-lag (T) fuses rather than fast-blow (F) fuses of the same current rating? Show answer

Motors draw much more current when starting than when running. An AC induction motor or universal motor can draw 3–8 times its running current for the first fraction of a second to second of startup, as it accelerates the rotor from rest against inertia. This startup current is brief and normal — it stops once the motor is up to speed. A fast-blow fuse rated for the running current would see this transient and blow, even though nothing is actually wrong. The time-lag (T) fuse has a thermal element that absorbs the brief energy pulse of startup current without melting, but will still blow on a sustained overcurrent (like a stalled motor or a winding fault). This is exactly the time-current characteristic needed for motor protection.

Q5 A vacuum cleaner keeps blowing its 3.15A T-rated fuse after a few minutes of running — but not on startup. The bag is empty, hose clear, filter clean. What does this pattern tell you, and where would you look? Show answer

The fuse surviving startup rules out a startup-current problem. Blowing after a few minutes of running means the motor is drawing an overcurrent that builds up over time — not a sudden short circuit. The T-rating absorbs brief transients but not sustained overcurrent, which is exactly what this is. The most likely cause: worn bearings that tighten as the motor heats up, forcing the motor to work harder and draw progressively more current. Alternatively, insulation in the winding may be failing as the motor heats, creating leakage or partial shorts between turns. Check the bearings by spinning the shaft when cold: should spin freely with no grinding or wobble. If the bearings feel fine, compare winding resistance cold and hot, and inspect for insulation damage or overheating signs. A large change or abnormal imbalance points to a winding fault.
